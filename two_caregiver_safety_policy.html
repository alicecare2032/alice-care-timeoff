Two-Caregiver & Bed-Bound Safety Policy

Alice Care places caregiver and client safety at the center of every shift. This policy specifies when one caregiver can safely staff a visit and when the shift requires two caregivers, sometimes paired with a transfer lift. Three factors drive the decision: client weight, mobility level, and bed-bound status. Bed-bound status automatically elevates risk regardless of weight, because most bed-bound tasks involve full-body handling. Whenever any factor crosses a threshold, the staffing model rises to match it. These rules protect caregivers from injury, protect clients from unsafe handling, and keep every visit aligned with our care standards.

Two Staffing Models at a Glance

Single Caregiver

The client weighs under 180 lbs, moves independently or with light assistance, and repositions without help. One trained caregiver covers the full shift safely.

Two Caregivers (Lift if Needed)

The client weighs 250 lbs or more, requires full-assist mobility, or cannot reposition in bed. A second caregiver joins the shift, and a transfer lift adds to the team for clients above 300 lbs or non-weight-bearing status.

What Defines a Bed-Bound Client

A client meets the bed-bound definition when any combination of these conditions holds. The intake team flags this status during enrollment and reviews it at every reassessment.

A Client is Bed-Bound When They

  • Remain in bed most or all of the day
  • Require assistance for every transfer
  • Cannot reposition independently
  • Use a hospital bed or similar medical setup

Mandatory Two-Caregiver Triggers (Bed-Bound)

The shift requires two caregivers if any one of these conditions applies:

  • The client cannot turn or reposition independently
  • The client requires a brief change in bed
  • The client requires boosting or pulling up in bed
  • The client holds non-weight-bearing status
  • The client shows resistance, stiffness, or pain during movement

Critical Task Guidance for Bed-Bound Care

Five core tasks shape every bed-bound shift. Each one carries its own staffing and equipment standard.
1
Repositioning in Bed
Caregivers never pull or drag a client alone. Two caregivers handle every side-to-side turn and every boost up in bed.
  • Use draw sheets and slide sheets to reduce friction
  • Coordinate the count out loud before each move
  • Stop the moment the client braces, stiffens, or guards
2
Brief Changes and Hygiene
Brief changes combine lifting and rolling. Two caregivers staff every change when the client cannot assist or the client's weight exceeds safe handling for one person.
  • One caregiver stabilizes the client's body
  • The second caregiver performs the hygiene step
  • The team protects dignity by keeping the client covered between steps
3
Pressure Injury Prevention
Caregivers reposition the client every two hours, or follow the schedule in the care plan when it specifies a different interval.
  • Use pillows and wedges to offload heels, hips, and shoulders
  • Avoid friction and shear by lifting rather than dragging
  • Document skin changes immediately and notify the office
4
Bed-to-Chair Transfers
If the team attempts a transfer: two caregivers staff the move, and a Hoyer lift carries the client whenever the client cannot assist.
  • Cancel the transfer when the equipment is unavailable
  • Cancel the transfer when the client refuses or shows distress
  • Document the cancellation and notify the office before the next shift
5
Feeding and Hydration in Bed
One caregiver delivers feeding and hydration when the client maintains an upright position without help.
  • Upgrade to two caregivers when the client needs positioning support
  • Upgrade to two caregivers when poor positioning raises aspiration risk
  • Stop feeding immediately at any sign of choking or coughing

Support Equipment Catalog and Staffing Impact

Equipment availability directly affects staffing decisions. Per the VA Safe Patient Handling Algorithms (the gold standard adopted across U.S. healthcare), the right device transforms many two-caregiver tasks into safe one-caregiver tasks. The following catalog covers every device Alice Care recognizes, the scenarios where each one applies, and the staffing reduction the device unlocks.
Equipment Use Case Staffing Effect
Floor lift (Hoyer or full-body sling lift) Hydraulic or electric lift with a full-body sling. Carries 100% of client weight. Use for non-weight-bearing clients and bariatric transfers above 300 lbs. 2 Caregivers + Lift   Per NIOSH guidance, two caregivers operate the lift for non-weight-bearing residents (one age 18+ runs the lift, the second positions the client). For clients who can actively assist, a single caregiver may operate the lift on a case-by-case basis.
Ceiling-mounted lift (track system) Permanently installed overhead track lift. The most ergonomically protective option per the OSHA Hospital eTool. Eliminates pushing and pulling load that floor lifts impose. 1 Caregiver Possible   A cooperative dependent client transfers safely with one caregiver per VA Safe Patient Handling Algorithms.
Sit-to-stand device (powered or manual) For partially weight-bearing clients who can sit at the edge of the bed and bend at hips, knees, and ankles per OSHA Hospital eTool definition. Eliminates manual lifting in transfers. 1 Caregiver   A partial-assist cooperative client transfers safely with one caregiver and the device.
Gait belt (transfer belt with handles) Stabilization aid for ambulatory and partial-assist clients. OSHA classifies the belt as a balance support, never a lifting device. 1 Caregiver   A cooperative partial-assist client supports stand-and-pivot transfers with one caregiver. Never used for lifting.
Slide sheets and friction-reducing devices Low-friction sheets for lateral transfers and bed repositioning. Reduce caregiver biomechanical load by 60 to 80% per the 2024 Davis review of safe patient handling biomechanics. Reduces Risk Level   Required for safe in-bed boosting. Two caregivers still apply for cannot-reposition bed-bound clients.
Adjustable hospital bed Height-adjustable bed with rails. Eliminates back strain during bedside care. Standard for Bed-Bound   Strongly recommended for every bed-bound client. Does not by itself change staffing count.
Lift chair recliner (powered) Electric recliner that tilts forward to assist sit-to-stand. For cooperative clients with partial weight-bearing capacity within the chair's rated weight limit. 1 Caregiver   The chair handles part of the lift load; the caregiver supervises and steadies.

Lift Certification: Authorized Solo-Operation Override

A lead caregiver who holds current Hoyer or mechanical lift competency, plus documented field experience with the specific client, can safely operate the lift solo for cooperative clients. This certification override allows Alice Care to send one caregiver instead of two, replacing the standard NIOSH-derived two-caregiver lift staffing for qualifying scenarios.

Certification Eligibility Checklist

  • Current competency on file. The lead caregiver holds a current Hoyer, sit-to-stand, or mechanical lift certification, renewed within the training cycle the office maintains.
  • Documented client-specific experience. The caregiver has completed at least three supervised shifts with this specific client, with each shift signed off by a lead caregiver or the office.
  • Cooperative client. The client follows directions reliably and shows no resistance, stiffness, or pain during lift operation.
  • Office written authorization. The office signs a case-specific authorization that names the certified caregiver, the client, the device, and the effective date range.
  • Lift in working order with rated capacity. The mechanical lift sits on site, charged or operational, with a rated weight capacity exceeding the client's weight by the manufacturer's safety margin.
  • Emergency escalation path documented. The caregiver carries the office on-call number and a clearly written escalation protocol for any in-shift difficulty.
Important Limits. The certification override does not apply when:
  • The client weighs above 300 lbs. Bariatric clients require two caregivers plus a lift regardless of certification, because the bariatric weight class introduces positioning demands beyond lift operation alone.
  • The client is non-cooperative, shows resistance, or has unpredictable movement patterns.
  • The certified caregiver is replaced for the shift. Substitutes who lack the same documented client-specific experience trigger an immediate revert to two-caregiver staffing.
  • The client experiences hospitalization, weight change above 10 lbs, or any reportable change in condition. Reassessment is required before the next certified shift.
Per NIOSH guidance, two caregivers operating a mechanical lift remains the default standard for non-weight-bearing residents. The certification override is a documented, named exception to this default; it is not a waiver of the underlying safety principle.

Equipment Verification Protocol

Before assigning the shift, the office confirms each item below for every client whose care plan requires equipment. Caregivers report missing or non-functional equipment immediately and pause the relevant task until the office resolves the gap.

Pre-Shift Equipment Verification

  • Equipment present. The required device sits on site at the start of the shift.
  • Working order. Powered devices charged or plugged in; manual devices free of damage; slings inspected for wear.
  • Weight capacity matches. The rated capacity of the device exceeds the client's weight by the manufacturer's safety margin.
  • Caregiver competency. The assigned caregiver holds current training documentation for the device on file.
  • Family briefed (if family-assisted). A capable family member acting as second caregiver has reviewed device operation with the lead caregiver.
  • Space and access. The home layout accommodates the device safely (door widths, floor type, room size for a Hoyer base).

Equipment Standard for Bed-Bound Clients

For every bed-bound client, the office verifies the equipment below before assigning the shift. Caregivers report any missing item before performing the affected task.

Strongly Recommended

  • Slide sheets and draw sheets
  • Positioning wedges and pillows
  • Adjustable hospital bed
  • Gait belt for any partial-assist transfers

Required When Applicable

  • Mechanical lift (floor or ceiling) for any transfer when the client cannot assist
  • Mechanical lift for any client above 300 lbs
  • Mechanical lift for non-weight-bearing clients
  • Sit-to-stand device for partial-assist transfers when available
  • Protective equipment for hygiene and brief changes

Caregiver Safety Authority and Core Rules

Caregivers hold the final word on field safety. The four rules below override any prior schedule, prior plan, or family request.
1
Stop immediately when a task feels unsafe. Caregiver instinct catches what charts miss. Document the concern, call the office, and request a reassessment before the next visit.
2
Never force movement. Resistance, stiffness, or pain stops the task. The team escalates instead of pushing through.
3
Use proper equipment for every high-risk transfer. Hoyer lifts, sit-to-stand devices, slide sheets, draw sheets, and gait belts protect both bodies in the room. Equipment availability sits inside the safety plan, not outside it.
4
Escalate to the Care Team for every doubt. The office adjusts staffing, dispatches a second caregiver, or schedules equipment delivery before the next shift. Caregivers carry no penalty for raising a concern.

Care Plan and Intake Documentation

Every bed-bound client carries a documented profile in the care plan. The intake team confirms each item below before the first shift and reviews it at every reassessment.

Required Intake Fields for Bed-Bound Clients

  • Mobility level. None, limited, or partial. The intake team selects one and notes triggers that change the level.
  • Repositioning frequency. Every 2 hours by default, or the interval the care plan specifies.
  • Toileting method. Brief change, or external catheter management only. The team flags any other method for medical scope review.
  • Equipment available on site. Slide sheets, draw sheets, wedges, hospital bed, and mechanical lift each receive a yes or no entry.
  • Staffing designation. One caregiver (1:1) or two caregivers (2:1). The plan documents the trigger that justifies the designation.
  • Reassessment trigger. Any weight change above 10 lbs, any new equipment need, any caregiver-reported safety concern, or any hospitalization triggers an immediate reassessment.
The Field Rule
If the client is in bed and cannot help you move them, you do not move them alone.

When the Care Plan Must Update

Client weight, mobility, repositioning ability, and skin condition shift over time. Caregivers report any change immediately so Alice Care reviews the staffing model, adjusts the care plan, adds a second caregiver, or schedules equipment delivery before the next shift. A care plan written six months ago does not bind today's safety decision. Caregiver judgment in the field overrides any prior schedule until the office completes the reassessment.

Best Practice and Compliance Alignment: Alice Care operates under CDSS License #344700080. The thresholds in this policy align with the safe-handling standards published by the agencies below.
  • Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA): avoid manual lifting and high-force exertion.
  • National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health (NIOSH): dependent patients require assistive devices or team handling, with a single-worker manual lift cap near 35 lbs in patient handling.
  • American Nurses Association (ANA): no-lift standards and team-based handling for dependent clients.
  • Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC): pressure injury prevention through scheduled repositioning.
  • California Department of Social Services (CDSS): non-medical scope and safety boundaries under the Home Care Services Consumer Protection Act (AB 1217), codified at California Health & Safety Code §§ 1796.10 to 1796.63.
