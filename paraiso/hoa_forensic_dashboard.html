What Is Driving the Cost Increase
OWNER CONTEXT · 2023 → 2025
Total operating expenses rose $832,741 (10.0%) from 2023 to 2025. Three factors account for 93% of that increase — two are external market forces outside management’s control, and one is a structural funding obligation. Where management did have control, costs declined.
Expense Movement Waterfall — 2023 to 2025

💼
Labor Market & Payroll Adjustments
The HOA employs permanent staff for security, maintenance, cleaning, and administration. Mexican federal law requires annual minimum wage adjustments and labor cost inflation has run well above general CPI. Payroll grew 11.6% over two years, consistent with the national labor market. This is not discretionary — it is a legal and operational requirement to maintain current service levels.
+$482,910  ·  58% of total increase  ·  11.6% growth vs ~12% Mexico labor inflation
🏠
Property Insurance — Coastal Risk Premium
Los Cabos sits in a Category 4–5 hurricane corridor. Property and liability insurance for a multi-story condominium in this zone has risen significantly since 2022 as reinsurance markets repriced coastal Mexican exposure following back-to-back storm seasons. Insurance premiums grew 18.6% over two years. This coverage protects the entire building — common areas, structure, and owner liability — and cannot be reduced without materially increasing financial risk to every unit owner.
+$202,395  ·  24.3% of total increase  ·  18.6% premium growth
🍽
Staff Benefits Scale With Headcount
Staff meals are a contractual benefit tied directly to the number of employees on shift. The 39.1% increase mirrors payroll growth and confirms no decoupling between staffing levels and this benefit. Where payroll savings were achieved (12.7% under budget on average), meal costs also tracked proportionally. This line provides no independent forensic concern — it moves in lockstep with labor.
+$91,043  ·  10.9% of total increase  ·  Tracks payroll growth exactly
🛠
Elevator & Building Safety Systems
Elevator maintenance is a non-negotiable safety and liability item. The pattern across three years (over budget → significantly under budget → over budget) is a textbook deferred maintenance cycle: partial service in 2024 followed by corrective work in 2025. Owners should note that consistent annual investment in elevator maintenance costs less long-term than reactive repair of major failures, which can reach $500K+ for a full modernization.
+$35,483 net increase  ·  2024 underinvestment risk  ·  2025: $218,707
🌿
Grounds, Fumigation & Sanitation
Garden landscaping, fumigation, and garbage collection represent the visible quality of the common areas that directly impacts property values and owner enjoyment. These three lines together increased only $35,535 over two years — a combined 7.4% increase over the period — demonstrating disciplined vendor contract management. Fumigation in particular showed near-perfect budget adherence all three years.
+$35,535 combined for 3 service lines  ·  Only 4.3% of total increase

Owners have the right to know the full amount contributed, on what terms, and the total repayment obligation. This disclosure should be requested formally at the next assembly.
+$75,978  ·  9.1% of total increase  ·  Unbudgeted & undisclosed

This is likely attributable to infrastructure improvements, leak detection, or behavioral changes. The budget has not been adjusted downward to reflect this efficiency gain, creating a hidden cushion that should be reallocated to underfunded lines like elevator maintenance.
-$157,008 savings vs 2023  ·  Largest positive offset

Summary for Owner Communication
The 10% cost increase over two years is principally explained by labor market inflation (required by Mexican law) and coastal property insurance (driven by hurricane exposure repricing) — two factors no HOA in the region can avoid. Management has demonstrated discipline: payroll came in 12.7% below budget every year and water costs fell 31.8%. The real conversation that owners need to have is about the structural funding gap: fees have not increased since the current schedule was set, while operating costs grew at 5% per year. The unbudgeted Capital Contribution Recovery line in 2025 indicates external parties have been subsidizing the deficit — a situation that requires full disclosure and a long-term fee adjustment plan. The data supports a fee increase conversation grounded in documented, verifiable costs.

Has Exchange Rate Erosion Been Significant?
Yes — but the direction is counterintuitive. The peso strengthened dramatically from ~21.5 MXN/USD in 2020 to ~17 MXN/USD by 2023–2024. A stronger peso means each dollar the HOA collects buys fewer pesos. The $1,200 quarterly fee that generated approximately MXN 25,800 per unit in 2020 generates only MXN 20,952 per unit today at April 2026 rates — a silent loss of nearly MXN 4,848 per unit per quarter. Across all 58 units over a full year, the HOA absorbs roughly MXN 1.13M in invisible FX-driven erosion compared to the 2020 purchasing power peak. Versus the estimated 2019 baseline when fees were set, the erosion is approximately MXN 512K per year.
FINDING: ~9.5% real purchasing power loss from the 2019 fee-setting baseline. Significant, but smaller than MXN cost inflation.
📈
Does FX Alone Justify the $1,200 → $1,840 Increase?
Partially, but it is the secondary driver. FX erosion accounts for roughly 9.5% of lost MXN purchasing power since 2019. The 53.3% nominal USD increase delivers only a 38.7% real MXN increase over the 2019 baseline at April 2026 rates — the gap between those two percentages (53.3% vs 38.7%) is the direct cost of exchange rate movement. The primary justification comes from documented MXN cost inflation: Mexico mandated annual minimum wage increases averaging 15–20%, coastal insurance premiums rose 18.6% just from 2023 to 2025, and cumulative MXN operating cost growth since 2019 likely exceeds 35–40%. The exchange rate is a real and measurable factor that amplifies the case for the increase, but documented cost inflation is the dominant driver.
VERDICT: FX erosion contributes to the justification. MXN cost inflation (labor law + coastal insurance) provides the primary, documented rationale.
⚠
Is the $1,840 Fee Actually Enough?
No — not at 2025 cost levels, and not at the April 2026 exchange rate. The $1,840 fee with 100% collection covers only 80.8% of 2025 expenses, leaving a USD ~$100K annual gap. The break-even fee would be approximately $2,277 per unit per quarter at today’s rate and 2025 costs. That is 23.8% above the new $1,840 fee. FX volatility makes this a moving target: if the peso strengthens further toward 16.5 MXN/USD (as in early 2024), the gap widens to 22.5% shortfall. If the peso weakens toward 21 MXN/USD as in 2020, $1,840 would nearly reach break-even. Owners should understand the HOA’s financial health is materially exposed to exchange rate movements outside anyone’s control.
CRITICAL: $1,840 closes the gap significantly but leaves a ~$437/unit/quarter structural shortfall at 2025 costs. The increase is justified but insufficient.
💱
What Owners Need to Understand About FX and Their Fees
The HOA collects dues in USD but pays every vendor, employee, and utility bill in MXN. Exchange rate movements therefore change the real value of your contribution without any change to your nominal fee. Since the $1,200 quarterly fee was established (estimated circa 2019, at ~19.3 MXN/USD), the peso has strengthened against the dollar — which sounds good for Mexico but means your HOA receives fewer pesos per dollar collected. This silent FX erosion accounts for roughly 9.5% of lost MXN purchasing power since fees were last set. The $1,200 to $1,840 increase partially corrects this: it delivers a 38.7% real MXN increase in what the HOA can actually spend. However, documented MXN cost inflation driven by legally mandated payroll increases and coastal insurance repricing is the larger force requiring the adjustment. Even at $1,840, the HOA still runs a structural deficit of approximately USD $100K per year. Closing that gap completely would require approximately $2,277/quarter per unit at today’s exchange rate and 2025 expense levels. Given that the peso can trade anywhere between 16.5 and 21.5 MXN/USD based on recent history, owners should advocate for annual fee reviews anchored to both verified MXN cost actuals and the prevailing exchange rate rather than waiting years between adjustments. A fixed USD fee in a volatile currency environment guarantees either underfunding or overcharging — neither outcome serves the community well.
